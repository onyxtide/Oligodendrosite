Neuron Structure: The Building Block of the Nervous System

Click on any part of the neuron to learn more Tap neuron parts to learn more

Key Neuron Structures

Neurons are specialized cells that transmit information throughout the body. While neurons are the primary signaling units, they work alongside glial cells that actively support and modulate their function. Click on any part in the diagram or cards below to explore in detail!

🌿

Dendrites

Signal Reception

Branched cytoplasmic extensions containing ribosomes, rough ER, and receptor proteins. They serve as the primary signal reception sites for neural communication.

Key Functions
  • • Bind neurotransmitters via receptors
  • • Generate graded potentials (EPSPs/IPSPs)
  • • Spatial and temporal summation
  • • Signal attenuation with distance
Clinical Significance

Dendritic spine loss occurs in Alzheimer's disease, schizophrenia, and depression. Spine morphology changes with learning and memory formation.

Medical Details

Contains ionotropic (AMPA, NMDA, GABA) and metabotropic receptors. Typical dendritic tree can receive 1,000-10,000 synaptic inputs.

🔬

Soma (Cell Body)

Metabolic Center

The metabolic center containing nucleus, Nissl bodies (rough ER), Golgi apparatus, and mitochondria. Houses genetic material and coordinates cellular activities.

Key Functions
  • • Protein synthesis via Nissl bodies
  • • Spatial/temporal signal integration
  • • ATP production in mitochondria
  • • Gene expression regulation
Clinical Significance

Chromatolysis (Nissl body dispersal) indicates neuronal injury. Central target for neurotoxins and therapeutic agents.

Medical Details

Diameter: 5-100μm. Contains high concentration of ribosomes for rapid protein synthesis. Integration occurs at axon hillock with threshold at ~-55mV.

📡

Axon

Signal Transmission

Long projection that conducts action potentials. Can extend from micrometers to over 1 meter in humans.

Key Functions
  • • Action potential conduction
  • • Axonal transport
  • • Signal propagation
Clinical Notes

Axonal injury leads to Wallerian degeneration.

🧈

Myelin Sheath

Insulation Layer

Lipid-rich insulation formed by oligodendrocytes (CNS) or Schwann cells (PNS). Increases conduction speed up to 50× via saltatory conduction. Increasingly recognized as a dynamic structure that adapts with learning.

Key Functions
  • • Electrical insulation
  • • Saltatory conduction
  • • Metabolic support
Clinical Notes

Myelin dysfunction contributes to multiple sclerosis and Guillain-Barré syndrome. Recent research also reveals myelin as a dynamic learning mechanism. See the Modern Discoveries tab.

💊

Axon Terminals

Neurotransmitter Release

Specialized endings containing synaptic vesicles with neurotransmitters. Release occurs via calcium-triggered exocytosis.

Key Functions
  • • Neurotransmitter release
  • • Synaptic transmission
  • • Chemical signaling
Clinical Notes

Target of psychiatric medications and botulinum toxin.

The Action Potential Process

1. Resting Potential (-70mV)

Membrane at equilibrium with selective permeability:

  • K⁺ leak channels (PK:PNa = 25:1 ratio)
  • Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pump (3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in)
  • Impermeable intracellular anions (A⁻)
  • Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz equation determines Em

2. Depolarization (Threshold: -55mV)

Voltage-gated Nav1.1-1.9 channels activate:

  • Positive feedback loop: Na⁺ influx → further depolarization
  • Peak at +30 to +40mV (ENa = +60mV)
  • Duration: 0.5-2ms (temperature dependent)
  • All-or-none (regenerative) response

3. Repolarization

Na⁺ inactivation and K⁺ channel opening:

  • Fast Na⁺ inactivation (h-gates close, τ = 0.1-0.2ms)
  • Delayed rectifier Kv channels (Kv1.1-1.8) activate
  • K⁺ efflux restores electronegativity
  • Membrane approaches EK (-90 to -100mV)

4. Afterhyperpolarization (AHP)

Prolonged K⁺ conductance creates undershoot:

  • Delayed rectifier K⁺ channels remain open
  • Ca²⁺-activated K⁺ channels (SK, BK) contribute
  • Membrane reaches -75 to -90mV
  • Duration: 2-5ms (varies by neuron type)

5. Recovery & Refractory Periods

Channel kinetics determine excitability:

  • Absolute refractory: Na⁺ channels inactivated (1-2ms)
  • Relative refractory: Higher threshold due to K⁺ conductance
  • Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase restores gradients (metabolically expensive)
  • Recovery from inactivation: τ = 1-10ms

Clinical Insights

Action potentials follow the "all-or-none" principle - once threshold (-55mV) is reached, the regenerative process is inevitable and stereotyped.

Medical relevance: Local anesthetics (lidocaine, procaine) block voltage-gated Na⁺ channels, preventing action potential propagation and eliminating pain sensation.

Neuron Types: Specialized for Different Functions

Functional Classification

Neurons are classified based on their function in the nervous system:

Sensory (Afferent) Neurons

Convert external stimuli into electrical signals for the brain. Found in sensory organs and the PNS.

Motor (Efferent) Neurons

Transmit signals from the CNS to muscles and glands to initiate actions and movements.

Interneurons

Connect other neurons within the CNS. They process and integrate information between sensory and motor neurons.

Structural Classification

Neurons can also be classified based on their structure:

Multipolar Neurons

Have one axon and multiple dendrites. Most common type in the CNS. Examples include motor neurons and interneurons.

Bipolar Neurons

Have one axon and one dendrite extending from opposite sides of the soma. Found in sensory pathways like the retina, inner ear, and olfactory system.

Unipolar Neurons

Have a single process that divides into two branches - one functions as a dendrite, the other as an axon. Common in sensory neurons of the PNS.

Function vs. Structure

While there's some correlation between a neuron's structure and function, they're not always directly related:

  • Sensory neurons are typically unipolar or bipolar
  • Motor neurons are generally multipolar
  • Interneurons are almost always multipolar

Nervous System Organization

Central vs Peripheral Nervous System

Central Nervous System (CNS)

Brain: Control center for all body functions, containing ~86 billion neurons

Spinal Cord: Information highway between brain and body, 31 segments

  • Protected by bone (skull & vertebrae)
  • Surrounded by cerebrospinal fluid
  • Blood-brain barrier protection

Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)

All neural tissue outside the CNS

  • 12 pairs of cranial nerves (from brain)
  • 31 pairs of spinal nerves (from spinal cord)
  • Ganglia: Clusters of neuron cell bodies
  • Sensory receptors throughout the body

Functional Divisions

Somatic Nervous System:

  • Voluntary motor control
  • Sensory information processing

Autonomic Nervous System:

  • Sympathetic (fight-or-flight)
  • Parasympathetic (rest-and-digest)
  • Enteric (gut nervous system)

Cranial Nerves: Direct Brain Connections

🧠 Your Brain's Direct Hotlines

Think of cranial nerves as dedicated phone lines between your brain and specific body parts. Unlike spinal nerves that take a detour through the spinal cord, these 12 pairs connect directly to your brainstem - like having a direct line to the CEO!

👁️ Special Senses (I, II, VIII)

Smell, vision, hearing, balance - your connection to the world

👀 Eye Movement (III, IV, VI)

Precise eye control for tracking, reading, depth perception

🗣️ Face & Voice (V, VII, IX, X, XI, XII)

Expression, speech, chewing, swallowing, autonomics

Each nerve has a unique personality and job description. Some are pure sensory (like your smell detector), others are pure motor (like your tongue controller), and some are mixed (doing multiple jobs). Click on any cranial nerve below to explore detailed anatomy and clinical correlations!

👃

I. Olfactory

Smell Sensation

Think of it as: The brain's "wireless antenna" for chemical signals. Unlike other cranial nerves, this one connects your nose directly to memory and emotion centers, explaining why smells trigger powerful memories!

🌸 How It Works
  • • Odorant molecules bind to receptors
  • • Signal travels through bone holes (cribriform plate)
  • • Bypasses thalamus (unique!)
  • • Direct route to memory centers
🧪 Unique Features
  • • Only nerve that regenerates continuously
  • • Neurons replace every 30-60 days
  • • No myelin sheath (unmyelinated)
  • • Shortest distance to brain
🔍 Clinical Conditions
  • • COVID-19: viral damage to receptors
  • • Head trauma: shearing of nerve fibers
  • • Alzheimer's: early olfactory changes
  • • Parkinson's: loss precedes motor symptoms
🩺 Clinical Pearl

Memory trick: Test with coffee, peppermint, or vanilla - but never use ammonia or alcohol (these stimulate pain receptors via CN V, not smell!). Loss of smell often predicts neurodegenerative disease by years.

👁️

II. Optic

Vision

Think of it as: Your brain's high-speed internet cable! Actually part of the brain (CNS) that extends into your eye. Each optic nerve contains 1.2 million "wires" carrying visual data at 120 mph!

👁️ Vision Highway
  • • Light hits retina (like camera sensor)
  • • 126 million photoreceptors convert to electrical signals
  • • Optic chiasm: images cross over (left sees right world)
  • • Visual cortex: brain "develops the photo"
🧠 Brain Connection
  • • Actually brain tissue (white matter)
  • • Covered by meninges
  • • Bathed in cerebrospinal fluid
  • • Cannot regenerate if cut
⚠️ Warning Signs
  • • Papilledema: brain pressure rising
  • • Sudden vision loss: stroke/inflammation
  • • Gradual loss: glaucoma "silent thief"
  • • Field cuts: specific brain lesions
🔍 Clinical Detective Work

Localization: Right visual field loss = left brain problem. Bitemporal hemianopia = pituitary tumor pressing chiasm. Swollen optic disc + headache = brain pressure emergency!

👀

III. Oculomotor

Eye Movement & Pupil

Mixed nerve controlling four of six extraocular muscles, pupillary constriction, and eyelid elevation through somatic and parasympathetic components.

Innervation
  • • Superior, inferior, medial recti
  • • Inferior oblique muscle
  • • Levator palpebrae superioris
  • • Pupillary sphincter (parasympathetic)
Clinical Signs
  • • Ptosis (drooping eyelid)
  • • Diplopia (double vision)
  • • Mydriasis (dilated pupil)
  • • "Down and out" eye position
Clinical Pearls

"Pupil-sparing" third nerve palsy suggests medical causes (diabetes). "Pupil-involving" suggests surgical emergency (aneurysm).

🔄

IV. Trochlear

Eye Rotation (Intorsion)

The smallest cranial nerve controlling the superior oblique muscle. Unique in being the only completely decussated (crossed) cranial nerve and having the longest intracranial course.

Anatomy
  • • Originates in dorsal midbrain
  • • Completely decussates
  • • Longest intracranial course
  • • Passes through trochlea (pulley)
Function
  • • Primary: Intorsion (inward rotation)
  • • Secondary: Depression when adducted
  • • Maintains torsional alignment
  • • Prevents diplopia
Clinical Signs
  • • Head tilt (compensatory)
  • • Diplopia (especially downward gaze)
  • • Hypertropia (eye elevation)
  • • Excyclotorsion
Clinical Testing

Test with Parks-Bielschowsky 3-step test. Patient looks down and in toward affected side. Most vulnerable to head trauma due to long course.

😬

V. Trigeminal

Facial Sensation & Chewing

Think of it as: Your face's security system! This nerve monitors everything touching your face and controls your jaw muscles. It's like having three security cameras (V1, V2, V3) covering different zones of your face.

👁️ V1 - "Upper Zone"
  • • Forehead & scalp (like wearing a headband)
  • • Upper eyelid protection
  • • Corneal touch → instant blink
  • • Inside of nose
😊 V2 - "Middle Zone"
  • • Cheek & upper lip (mustache area)
  • • Upper teeth (dentist territory!)
  • • Maxillary sinus
  • • Side of nose
💪 V3 - "Power Zone"
  • • Lower jaw & chin (goatee area)
  • • Lower teeth & tongue side
  • • MOTOR: Chewing muscles
  • • Only division that moves muscles!
⚡ Trigeminal Neuralgia - "Lightning in the Face"

Most painful condition known! Triggered by light touch (shaving, brushing teeth). Pain like electric shock lasting seconds. Often caused by blood vessel pressing on nerve root.

↔️

VI. Abducens

Lateral Eye Movement

Pure motor nerve controlling the lateral rectus muscle, responsible for outward eye movement (abduction). Has the longest subarachnoid course, making it vulnerable to increased intracranial pressure.

Anatomical Course
  • • Originates: Pontomedullary junction
  • • Course: Longest subarachnoid path
  • • Target: Lateral rectus muscle only
  • • Vulnerable to: ↑ ICP, aneurysms
Clinical Presentation
  • • Lateral rectus palsy
  • • Esotropia (inward eye turn)
  • • Horizontal diplopia
  • • Cannot abduct affected eye
Localization Value

Isolated sixth nerve palsy often indicates increased ICP (false localizing sign). In children, may be first sign of brain tumor. Test by asking patient to look laterally.

😊

VII. Facial

Facial Expression & Taste

Complex nerve with motor, sensory, and parasympathetic components controlling facial expression, taste, and salivary/lacrimal secretions.

Functions
  • • Facial expression muscles
  • • Taste (anterior 2/3 tongue)
  • • Submandibular/sublingual glands
  • • Lacrimal gland (tears)
Bell's Palsy
  • • Unilateral facial weakness
  • • Cannot close eye/smile
  • • Loss of taste
  • • Hyperacusis (loud sounds)
Clinical Testing

Facial symmetry, eye closure, smile, taste testing. Upper vs. lower motor neuron patterns distinguish central vs. peripheral lesions.

👂

VIII. Vestibulocochlear

Hearing & Balance

Sensory nerve with two distinct components: cochlear (hearing) and vestibular (balance). Critical for auditory processing and spatial orientation.

Cochlear Division
  • • Hair cells in organ of Corti
  • • Tonotopic organization
  • • Spiral ganglion neurons
  • • Frequency discrimination 20Hz-20kHz
Vestibular Division
  • • 3 semicircular canals (rotation)
  • • Utricle & saccule (linear acceleration)
  • • Vestibular ganglion
  • • Vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR)
Hearing Disorders

Sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, acoustic neuroma. Weber and Rinne tests differentiate conductive vs. sensorineural loss.

Balance Disorders

Vertigo, nystagmus, ataxia. Head impulse test, Dix-Hallpike maneuver, electronystagmography for assessment.

👅

IX. Glossopharyngeal

Taste & Swallowing

Mixed nerve with sensory, motor, and parasympathetic components. Essential for swallowing, taste from posterior tongue, and carotid body/sinus regulation.

Sensory Functions
  • • Taste: posterior 1/3 tongue
  • • General sensation: pharynx, tonsils
  • • Carotid body chemoreceptors
  • • Carotid sinus baroreceptors
Motor Function
  • • Stylopharyngeus muscle
  • • Assists in swallowing
  • • Pharyngeal elevation
  • • Works with CN X
Parasympathetic
  • • Parotid gland secretion
  • • Via otic ganglion
  • • Salivary reflexes
  • • Gustatory sweating
Clinical Assessment

Gag reflex (afferent limb), taste testing posterior tongue, parotid gland function. Glossopharyngeal neuralgia causes severe throat pain triggered by swallowing.

🫀

X. Vagus

Parasympathetic Control

Think of it as: Your body's "cruise control" system! The vagus nerve is like the commander of your "rest and digest" mode, telling your organs to slow down, relax, and focus on maintenance instead of emergency response.

💓 Heart & Lungs
  • • Heart: "Slow down, you're safe"
  • • Breathing: Deeper, calmer breaths
  • • Blood pressure: "Relax those vessels"
  • • Stress response: "Cancel the alarm"
🍽️ Digestion HQ
  • • Stomach: "Start the acid, churn the food"
  • • Intestines: "Mix and move everything along"
  • • Liver: "Release the digestive juices"
  • • Why you're sleepy after big meals!
🗣️ Voice & Throat
  • • Vocal cords: Controls your voice pitch
  • • Swallowing: Coordinates the complex dance
  • • Gag reflex: "Is this safe to swallow?"
  • • Throat surgery risk: Can damage voice
🧘 Modern Medicine: Hacking the Vagus

Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VNS): Implanted device "exercises" the vagus to treat epilepsy and depression. Deep breathing, meditation, and cold exposure naturally strengthen vagal tone = better stress resilience!

💪

XI. Accessory (Spinal)

Neck & Shoulder Muscles

Pure motor nerve with unique spinal origin. Controls sternocleidomastoid and trapezius muscles, essential for head rotation and shoulder elevation.

Anatomy
  • • Origin: Spinal cord C1-C5 segments
  • • Unique ascending course through foramen magnum
  • • Joins CN X briefly, then separates
  • • No cranial nucleus component
Functions
  • • Sternocleidomastoid: head rotation
  • • Trapezius: shoulder elevation/shrug
  • • Neck stabilization
  • • Accessory respiratory muscle
Clinical Testing

Test head rotation against resistance, shoulder shrug. Ask patient to turn head to opposite side while examiner resists.

Clinical Conditions

Spinal accessory nerve palsy: dropped shoulder, weak head turn. Often iatrogenic from lymph node biopsy or neck surgery.

👅

XII. Hypoglossal

Tongue Movement

Pure motor nerve controlling intrinsic and extrinsic tongue muscles. Essential for speech articulation, swallowing, and tongue movement in all directions.

Innervated Muscles
  • • Genioglossus (protrusion)
  • • Hyoglossus (retraction, depression)
  • • Styloglossus (retraction, elevation)
  • • Intrinsic muscles (shape changes)
Clinical Signs
  • • Tongue deviation (toward lesion)
  • • Dysarthria (speech difficulty)
  • • Dysphagia (swallowing difficulty)
  • • Fasciculations (lower motor neuron)
Clinical Testing

Tongue protrusion (deviates toward weak side), lateral movement, strength against cheek. Observe for atrophy or fasciculations.

Localization

Upper motor neuron: tongue deviates away from lesion. Lower motor neuron: tongue deviates toward lesion side.

Clinical Memory Aids

🔬 Modern Neuroscience: Beyond the Classical View

Neuroscience has been transformed by discoveries in the last decade. While the fundamentals covered in this chapter remain essential, researchers have uncovered entirely new dimensions of how the brain works. Here's what the latest science reveals.

🧩

Glia: The Brain's Hidden Majority

Once dismissed as "glue," glial cells are now recognized as active participants in nearly every brain function

Astrocytes: The Third Synaptic Partner

The "tripartite synapse" model (Araque et al., 1999) established that astrocytes actively wrap around synapses and regulate neurotransmission. Recent 2025-26 studies show astrocytes:

  • Release gliotransmitters (ATP, glutamate, D-serine) that modulate synaptic strength
  • Control brain state transitions, arousal, attention, and even behavioral persistence (Freeman et al., 2025)
  • Synthesize cholesterol needed for synapse formation and maintenance
  • Clear extracellular glutamate via transporters, protecting neurons from excitotoxicity

🧈 Oligodendrocytes: Your Brain's Namesake

Oligodendrocytes do far more than insulate axons. Myelin plasticity, meaning experience-dependent changes to myelin, is now recognized as a fundamental learning mechanism:

  • Motor learning triggers new myelin formation within days (McKenzie et al., 2014)
  • Oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs) remain active throughout life, responding to neuronal activity
  • Dynamic myelination tunes conduction speed, synchronizing neural circuits
  • Myelin dysfunction is linked to cognitive impairment beyond demyelinating diseases
Microglia

The brain's resident immune cells. They prune synapses during development and continuously surveil for damage. Recent work shows they also shape learning-related synaptic changes.

NG2 Glia (OPCs)

A distinct class of glia that maintain proliferative capacity into adulthood. They receive direct synaptic input from neurons, a remarkable discovery that blurs the line between "neuron" and "glia."

Satellite Glia

In the PNS, satellite glia transfer mitochondria to neurons via tunneling nanotubes. This 2026 discovery suggests that glia literally fuel neurons (Nature, Jan 2026).

⚡

Myelin Plasticity: Learning Changes Brain Wiring

Myelin is not just static insulation; it's a dynamic learning mechanism

Classical neuroscience taught that myelin simply speeds up signal transmission. We now know myelin actively adapts with experience:

How Myelin Adapts
  • Activity-dependent myelination: Neuronal firing triggers OPC differentiation and new myelin formation
  • Conduction tuning: Adjusting myelin thickness and internode length synchronizes signals across circuits
  • Motor learning: Mice that learn new motor skills require new myelin; blocking myelination impairs learning
  • Continues into adulthood: Myelination peaks in the 20s but adaptive changes persist throughout life
Why It Matters
  • Explains why white matter changes are seen in learning disorders and cognitive decline
  • Multiple sclerosis is not just a myelin disease; it's a learning and circuit timing disease
  • Opens therapeutic avenues: Can we enhance myelination to support cognitive rehabilitation?
  • White matter plasticity may be as important as synaptic plasticity for complex learning
🌱

Adult Neurogenesis: Your Brain Keeps Making Neurons

A long debate, now resolved: adult humans do grow new neurons

The Resolution

For decades, the prevailing view was that "you are born with all the neurons you'll ever have." This is now known to be incomplete.

Using single-cell transcriptomics, Dumitru et al. (2025, Science) identified proliferating neural progenitor cells in the adult human hippocampus, confirming that new neurons are born throughout life, even into old age. Additional 2025-26 studies show "SuperAgers" maintain higher rates of neurogenesis well into their 80s.

Where It Happens

Primarily in the dentate gyrus of the hippocampus. New neurons integrate into existing circuits and contribute to pattern separation in memory.

What Influences It

Exercise, enriched environments, and learning promote neurogenesis. Chronic stress, aging, and neuroinflammation suppress it. Sleep quality also modulates progenitor cell activity.

Why It Matters

Adult neurogenesis is a form of structural plasticity that contributes to memory formation, mood regulation, and cognitive resilience. Its decline is associated with depression and cognitive aging.
